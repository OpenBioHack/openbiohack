## Example A — Tributyrin (gut; Category 2 — low-risk, may also act on a root cause)

*What it is.* Tributyrin is a molecule of glycerol joined to three molecules of butyrate — butyrate being a short-chain fatty acid that gut-lining cells use. It works as a slow-release way to deliver butyrate: your gut's fat-splitting enzymes (lipases) cut the butyrate free gradually as the dose travels along, which protects it from being destroyed in the stomach the way plain butyrate salts are.

*Where butyrate's best-understood benefit lives — and why it may not apply to you.* Butyrate's strongest, best-studied role is in the large intestine, where it is the main fuel for the cells lining the wall; those cells burn it using oxygen, and that oxygen-burning keeps the space just inside the wall (the lumen — the hollow channel gut contents pass through) low in oxygen, which holds back the oxygen-tolerant bacteria that tend to overgrow. The caveat for your case: that is a large-intestine mechanism. The cells lining the upper small intestine — where your picture points — don't run on butyrate; they run mainly on an amino acid called glutamine, and on glucose. So that "fuel the lining, lower the oxygen" benefit doesn't straightforwardly carry up to where your problem seems to sit.

*What could still plausibly help higher up — and the chemistry.* Two routes could in principle work in the upper small intestine. First, tightening the barrier: the lining cells are held together by protein seals called tight junctions (think of grouting between tiles, controlling what slips between the cells) built from proteins like claudin, occludin and ZO-1; butyrate can raise how much of these the cells make, by blocking a family of enzymes called histone deacetylases (HDACs) that normally keep the relevant genes switched off, and by activating an internal energy sensor (AMPK) that helps assemble the seals — a tighter seal means fewer inflammatory bacterial fragments cross to trigger the immune system behind it. Second, calming inflammation: butyrate docks onto receptors on gut and immune cells (GPR43, GPR41, GPR109A), nudging immune cells such as macrophages toward a calmer state and encouraging regulatory T cells (which damp inflammation, partly via a calming signal called interleukin-10), and the same HDAC-blocking turns down the cell's master inflammation switch, NF-κB.

*The dose question, done honestly (the speculative part — please question it).* It is tempting to answer this from blood tests, but that would be invalid: butyrate is absorbed and used up so fast by the gut wall and liver that the amount in blood (about fifty millionths of a mole per litre after tributyrin) says almost nothing about the amount in the gut channel itself — a low blood level is exactly what you'd see even if the gut-level concentration were high. The right way is to estimate from the dose and the fluid it dissolves into: a one-gram dose releases roughly four to five thousandths of a mole of butyrate in the small intestine, and the small intestine holds surprisingly little free fluid (often only tens of millilitres, in scattered pockets), so the amount released is on the order of forty-five times more than needed to reach the roughly one-millimolar concentration the barrier/anti-inflammatory effects require — realistic local levels are several to a few tens of millimolar. So reaching the needed concentration is plausibly not the problem; the catch is time — the wall absorbs butyrate within minutes, so any one stretch sees the high level only briefly. The genuine constraint is how long the exposure lasts, not how high it peaks. (Every number here is an estimate from dose, gut-fluid volumes and absorption rates — no one has measured upper-gut luminal butyrate after tributyrin in people — so this is transparent reasoning to question, not a measured fact.)

*What that implies.* If duration is the constraint, the levers that matter lengthen exposure rather than raise the peak: taking it with a fatty meal (recruits more fat-splitting enzymes and slows transit), slower transit in general, and slow-release formulations.

*Why for you specifically.* Given your current diet of mostly meat, fish and potato with little fermentable fibre, the fibre-eating bacteria in your colon that would normally make butyrate for you have little to feed on, so your own production is likely low — part of why supplying it directly is worth considering; broadening fibre to restart your own production is usually the better long-term route, taken up separately.

## Example B — Quercetin (skin / mast-cell; Category 2 — low-risk, aimed at a possible root cause)

*What it is and why it's here.* Quercetin is a plant flavonoid (a natural compound in onions, apples and capers), sold as a 500–1000 mg supplement. The reason to consider it isn't to block the itch the way an antihistamine does, but to try to calm the cells that drive the hives at their source — the mast cells.

*The mechanism.* The cells behind hives are mast cells, in the dermis (the layer just beneath the skin surface), clustered around the small blood vessels. When triggered they "degranulate" — dumping stored packets of histamine and other irritants into the tissue, producing the wheal, redness and itch. Added to human mast cells in the lab, quercetin genuinely quietens this: it cuts how much histamine they release and blocks the rise in calcium inside the cell that degranulation depends on (calcium is the trigger signal), and it turns down a master inflammation switch, NF-κB. So the biology is real, and for the histamine-release and calcium parts it's directly measured in human cells. (Less certain: whether this is even the dominant trigger in chronic hives, where the cause is often not the classic allergy pathway.)

*The catch — does a swallowed dose reach the cells strongly enough?* In those lab studies, the quieting effect needed quercetin at roughly 10 to 100 micromolar (a concentration — micromolar is millionths of a mole per litre) against human mast cells. The dose side, with the trap to avoid: it's tempting to compare total quercetin in blood to that bar, but that's two different things. First, quercetin is poorly absorbed and then almost entirely "wrapped" by the body — over 98% of what circulates is conjugated forms (a sugar-acid or sulfate group attached, mainly to excrete it), largely inactive at the mast cell because the wrapping stops them getting in. The active, unwrapped form (the aglycone) is only a few to a few tens of nanomolar in blood (nanomolar being thousandths of a micromolar). Second, the lab bar was set with the active form. Comparing active-available to active-needed: the active form sits roughly a thousand to ten thousand times below the concentration that works on human mast cells; even on the most generous assumptions it's about six to over a hundred times short.

*One real counter-argument, stated honestly.* At an inflamed spot, immune cells release an enzyme (beta-glucuronidase) that can unwrap some conjugated quercetin back to the active form locally — which genuinely pushes in quercetin's favour — but it probably can't close a thousand-fold gap (you can't unwrap more than the small local pool present, and no one has measured the active level in inflamed human skin). Daily dosing also builds the level up a few-fold, which doesn't bridge it either. (Estimates from absorption data and lab thresholds; the concentration actually reached at skin mast cells has never been measured in people — transparent reasoning, not a measured fact.)

*The honest verdict.* The mast-cell-calming mechanism is real in a dish, but at normal oral doses it most likely falls well short of the concentration it would need at the skin. It's low-risk and mechanistically motivated, so it's reasonable to consider — but as a long shot at standard doses, not as something shown to work in people. What would change that: a formulation that genuinely raises the active form by the needed amount, or a measurement of active quercetin in inflamed skin reaching the low-micromolar range.
