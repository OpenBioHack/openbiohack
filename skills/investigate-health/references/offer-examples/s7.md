# Offer-example library — Section 7: What knowing more would unlock

**Voice + bar (match this; do not copy the text).** Client-facing, plain-language, probabilistic. This section takes a key test or open question and lays out the path forward as a *branching tree*: it does not list "things to do," it branches on what the result could be, and for *each branch* it explains the mechanism — *why* that result could possibly suggest what it does. Define every term the first time it appears. Use "could possibly suggest" (never "points to"), "one thing that could be explored" (never "the first thing"). Nothing here is a diagnostic conclusion; a result narrows the field, it does not crown a winner — de-prioritised, not ruled out. Two worked examples across domains follow (gut; iron) — each is drawn as a tree with the mechanism written onto each branch, so the reasoning is shown landing both ways.

---

## Example A — The gut tree: a day of fasting × a breath-ethanol test

*Why this pair.* Your picture has two big open questions that a single set of cheap observations could narrow at once. First: is the gas mostly about **fuel** (the food coming in) or mostly about **clearance** (how well the gut sweeps and seals)? Second: are **yeast** part of the mix, or mainly bacteria? One short experiment speaks to the first; one breath test speaks to the second. Run together, they split the field into a small number of distinct directions, each with a different next move. None of this is a diagnosis — each branch is a lead to explore with your clinician.

*Two quick definitions before the tree.* The **"cleaning wave"** is the housekeeping contraction the small intestine runs between meals, sweeping leftover food and bacteria downstream; when it is weak, residue and the gas-makers linger. A **breath-ethanol test** measures alcohol on the breath after a sugar drink — useful because yeast, unlike most gut bacteria, make ethanol (alcohol) as their main product, so detecting it on the breath could possibly suggest yeast are present and active.

### The tree

**Branch 1 — A day of fasting drops the gas a lot.**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* food is the main **fuel** making the gas.
→ *The mechanism (why):* the gas is produced when microbes ferment a substrate. Take the food away for a day and you take away the substrate they ferment — if the gas falls with it, that is the signature of a fuel-led process, because removing the input removed the output.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* improving and speeding **digestion** so that food is broken down and absorbed higher up the small intestine. The reasoning: if more of a meal is absorbed early, less of it travels down to where the denser microbe populations sit, leaving them less to ferment. (Honest limit: this "absorb it earlier to starve them" logic holds cleanly only if the overgrowth sits lower down. If microbes are overgrown high up in the small intestine, they sit *alongside* the incoming food and intercept it early — so this lever weakens. Which is true for you is itself an open question, which is why it stays "explore," not "do.")

**Branch 2 — A day of fasting barely changes the gas.**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* the issue is less about **fuel** and more about **clearance**.
→ *The mechanism (why):* if removing the food doesn't remove the gas, the substrate isn't the bottleneck. What's left is the disposal side — gas and byproducts lingering because the cleaning wave is slow (so residue isn't swept downstream) or the barrier is leaky (so byproducts cross and irritate rather than being contained and cleared). The gas persisting through a fast is what a clearance-led process looks like.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* supporting the cleaning wave (the between-meal sweep) and the gut barrier, rather than chasing the diet alone — because on this branch, changing the fuel was already shown to move the needle very little.

### Now layer the breath-ethanol test across both branches

**Branch A — The breath-ethanol test is positive (ethanol detected).**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* **yeast** stay in the picture.
→ *The mechanism (why):* yeast's main fermentation product is ethanol. Most gut bacteria don't end up there — they make hydrogen, methane, or short-chain acids instead. So ethanol appearing on the breath after a sugar drink could possibly suggest yeast are present and actively fermenting, because the product is close to yeast-specific.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* a workup that includes yeast, not bacteria alone — the relevant point being that an approach aimed only at bacteria would, by design, leave yeast untouched.

**Branch B — The breath-ethanol test is negative (no ethanol).**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* yeast are a **less likely** major player. (De-prioritised, not ruled out — a single negative lowers the odds, it doesn't erase them.)
→ *The mechanism (why):* if active yeast were a big contributor, you'd expect their characteristic product to show up; not finding it lowers the likelihood that yeast are doing much of the work, and shifts weight toward the bacterial (and clearance) side of the picture.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* focusing first on the branch the fasting result picked out (fuel vs clearance), without spending effort on a yeast-directed step the evidence doesn't currently support.

*Reading the four corners together.* Fasting-drops-gas **+** ethanol-positive could possibly suggest a fuel-led picture with a yeast component (explore digestion *and* a yeast-inclusive workup). Fasting-barely-changes-gas **+** ethanol-negative could possibly suggest a clearance-led, bacteria-leaning picture (explore the sweep and the barrier). The other two corners sit in between. This is the whole point of branching the tests rather than running one: each combination narrows to a different next move — and each move is a possibility to discuss with your clinician, not a conclusion.

## Example B — The iron tree: ferritin read alongside CRP and transferrin saturation

*Why these together.* A low or high iron-storage number on its own is genuinely ambiguous, because the storage marker can be pushed around by something other than iron. Reading it *alongside* an inflammation marker and a "how loaded is the transport" marker is what turns an ambiguous number into a branch you can act on. As above, each branch is a lead to explore with a clinician, not a label.

*Definitions before the tree.* **Ferritin** is the protein iron is stored inside, so the blood level is normally used as a proxy for how much iron you have in the bank. **C-reactive protein (CRP)** is a marker of inflammation. **Transferrin saturation** is the percentage of the blood's iron-carrying protein (transferrin) that is actually loaded with iron — a "how full are the delivery trucks" reading. **Hepcidin** is the hormone that controls iron traffic, and **interleukin-6** is an inflammatory signalling molecule — both appear in the mechanism below.

### The tree

**Branch 1 — Ferritin is low AND CRP is normal.**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* **true iron deficiency**.
→ *The mechanism (why):* ferritin can be inflated by inflammation independently of how much iron you actually have. With CRP normal, there's no inflammatory signal doing that inflating — so a low ferritin can be read closer to face value as genuinely low stores, because the main confounder is absent.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* the **cause** of the low iron — whether it's loss (for example bleeding) or poor absorption — rather than just topping iron up, since a leak left unaddressed refills slowly.

**Branch 2 — Ferritin is high AND CRP is high AND transferrin saturation is low.**
→ *Could possibly suggest:* **anaemia of inflammation** rather than genuinely good stores.
→ *The mechanism (why):* the inflammatory signal interleukin-6 drives *two* things at once. It raises **ferritin** directly (which is why the storage number looks high), and it raises the hormone **hepcidin**, which traps iron inside storage cells and out of the blood — which is why the transport trucks read low (low saturation) at the same time. So a high ferritin *with* high CRP and low saturation is the fingerprint of iron being *hidden by inflammation*, not iron being plentiful: the same signal inflates the storage reading and lowers the circulating supply.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* the **inflammatory source** driving the signal — and it's worth noting that while hepcidin is high, swallowed (oral) iron is likely to be poorly absorbed, because hepcidin's whole job in this state is to keep iron from entering the blood. So "more oral iron" is the move this branch argues *against*, not for.

**Branch 3 — Ferritin is high AND CRP is normal.** (the in-between corner, for completeness)
→ *Could possibly suggest:* the high ferritin is less likely to be inflammation-driven, so other explanations for genuinely high iron move up the list.
→ *The mechanism (why):* with no inflammatory signal present to inflate it, the storage reading is harder to dismiss as a false high — so it's read closer to face value, the mirror image of Branch 1.
→ *One thing that could be explored:* a different workup entirely from Branch 2 — because here the question isn't "is iron hidden," it's "why might stored iron genuinely be elevated," which is a separate conversation to have with a clinician.

*Reading it together.* The single number — ferritin — means three different things depending on the two readings sitting next to it. That is exactly why the tree branches on the *combination*: the storage marker alone can't separate "truly low," "falsely high," and "genuinely high," but adding an inflammation marker and a transport-loading marker splits them apart, and each split has a different and sometimes opposite next step. None of these is a diagnosis — each is a direction the combined results could possibly suggest, to be confirmed and acted on with a clinician.

## What this section is for (note to the writer model)

1. A Section-7 entry is a **tree, not a list**. Pick a key test or open question, then branch on the possible results. Each branch carries three things: what the result *could possibly suggest*, the *mechanism* (why that result implies that — name the actor and the direction), and the *one thing that could be explored* if the branch holds.
2. Where two tests interact, **cross them** — show the corners, because the value is in the combination narrowing to distinct next moves, not in either test alone.
3. Keep the register probabilistic throughout: "could possibly suggest … because [mechanism], so one thing that could be explored is …". Never "points to," never "the first thing," never a diagnosis. A result **de-prioritises**, it does not rule out.
4. Always state the honest limit on a branch where one exists (for the gut tree: the "absorb earlier to starve them" lever depends on *where* the overgrowth sits). The limit is part of the mechanism, not an aside.
