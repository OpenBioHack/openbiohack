# Offer-example library — Section 1: Leading hypotheses

**Voice + bar (match this; do not copy the text).** Client-facing, plain-language, probabilistic. This is the headline section: a small, ranked set of leading hypotheses, each given (a) a band, (b) a plain-word statement of what it claims is happening in your body — the lived thing, not a syndrome label — and (c) a one-line hint at the mechanism underneath. The full step-by-step mechanism walk belongs to Section 2; here we only headline. Define every term the first time it appears. Nothing is stated as certain, nothing is "ruled out" — lower-ranked candidates are "de-prioritised". Two worked examples across body systems follow (gut; non-gut), each a ranked top-three with banded claims and a single mechanism hint apiece.

A note on the word **band**, used throughout. A band is a *coarse* likelihood — higher / moderate / lower — showing how we are prioritising the possibilities, not a measured probability. It is a way of ranking what to look at first, **not a diagnostic conclusion**. Where a rough percentage appears alongside a band, read it the same way: an ordering aid, not a measurement.

## Example A — Unexplained belching (gut)

The picture is frequent, excessive belching that doesn't track neatly with any one food. Three candidates, ranked. Each is a claim about what's physically happening, with one line on why — the detail comes in Section 2.

**1. Higher band — gas is being made unusually high up in the gut, then travelling back up.**
*The lived claim:* gas is being generated in the small intestine (the long upper tube between stomach and colon, where food is normally digested and absorbed, and which is normally kept fairly low in bacteria) rather than far down in the large intestine where most gut gas belongs — and because it's made high up, it surfaces as belching rather than passing downward.
*Mechanism hint:* certain gram-negative bacteria (a class of bacteria, named for how they stain, that tend to overgrow the upper gut when the normal controls slip) ferment carbohydrate that hasn't yet been absorbed, releasing gas right where it can rise back up. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

**2. Moderate band — the gas isn't over-produced so much as cleared too slowly.**
*The lived claim:* a normal amount of gas is being made, but it lingers because the gut isn't sweeping itself out between meals the way it should, so it accumulates and comes back up.
*Mechanism hint:* between meals the small intestine runs a periodic "housekeeper" wave — a cleaning sweep that clears residue and bacteria downstream every hour or two — and if that sweep is weak or infrequent, gas and the bugs that make it are left to build up. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

**3. Lower band — a bile-handling contribution.**
*The lived claim:* fat in a meal isn't being fully processed because of how bile (the digestive fluid the liver makes to break up fat) is delivered or handled, leaving more undigested material behind for gut bacteria to work on.
*Mechanism hint:* incompletely handled fat and bile leave extra substrate in the gut for bacteria to ferment, which can add to gas — a secondary contributor here rather than a main driver, which is why it sits lowest. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

*Why ranked this way, briefly.* The top two both explain "high-up gas that surfaces as belching" directly — one by making too much in the wrong place, one by clearing too little — so they lead. The bile route is plausible but a step removed from the belching itself, so it's de-prioritised, not dropped. Section 2 walks each chain; none of these is settled by reasoning alone, which is what the later sections are for.

## Example B — Unexplained fatigue with feeling cold all the time (non-gut)

The picture is persistent tiredness alongside cold intolerance — feeling cold when others are comfortable — with standard first-line thyroid screening that looked unremarkable. Three candidates, ranked.

**1. Higher band — the thyroid gland is fine, but the body isn't switching the storage form of the hormone into the active form.**
*The lived claim:* the gland is producing enough hormone, but the conversion that turns the mostly-inactive *storage* thyroid hormone (called T4) into the *active* one your tissues actually use (called T3) is running low — so the body behaves under-powered even though the gland and the usual screening test read normal.
*Mechanism hint:* most active T3 is made not in the thyroid but out in the body's tissues (especially the liver) by converter enzymes, and illness, stress, or inflammation can turn that conversion down — lowering active hormone while the standard screen still looks fine. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

**2. Moderate band — low iron availability limiting how much oxygen the blood can carry.**
*The lived claim:* there isn't enough usable iron on hand to carry oxygen efficiently, so tissues run short of oxygen and the result is fatigue and poor temperature regulation — and a "normal" iron-storage number can be misleading if there's inflammation in the background.
*Mechanism hint:* iron is needed to build the oxygen-carrying part of red blood cells, and the body's iron-control hormone can lock iron away during inflammation — so stores can read adequate while the iron that's actually reaching the blood is low. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

**3. Lower band — something else not yet on this list.**
*The lived claim:* the tiredness and cold could come from a cause outside the two above — for example sleep, mood, or another hormonal axis — that the current information isn't pointing to strongly yet.
*Mechanism hint:* fatigue and cold intolerance are non-specific (many different processes produce them), so an open lower-band slot is kept deliberately, to be promoted if new information points that way. (Band, not a diagnostic conclusion.)

*Why ranked this way, briefly.* The conversion candidate leads because it fits the specific combination — real symptoms *with* a normal-looking standard screen — better than the alternatives, since that screen can miss a conversion problem. The iron route is a strong, common, easily-checked second. The open "something else" slot sits lowest by design: it's a placeholder that keeps the set honest, not a worked-out claim. As above, Section 2 walks the mechanisms and the later sections cover what would actually distinguish them — none is decided here.
