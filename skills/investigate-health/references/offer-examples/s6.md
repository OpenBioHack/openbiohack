# Offer-example library — Section 6: Higher-risk options, and what to test first

**Voice + bar (match this; do not copy the text).** Client-facing, plain-language, probabilistic. Define every term the first time it appears. These are options that might act on a *root cause* (and sometimes on the symptom too) — but each carries a real downside: side-effects, something hard to undo, or a step up in intensity that can make things worse if aimed wrong. So the risk-versus-reward only makes sense *after* more is known. Each option is paired with the test or tests that would sensibly come first, split into cheap/at-home and more expensive/specialist. For every test, three things are stated in order: (1) the management decision its result would change; (2) the node — the step in the body's chain — it speaks to; (3) which version of the situation each possible result tilts toward, and *why* — the mechanism behind the tilt. Nothing here is a diagnostic conclusion, and nothing is an instruction; these are possibilities to weigh, and where a clinician's involvement is needed that is said plainly. Two worked examples across body systems follow (gut; thyroid).

---

## Example A — A course aimed at reducing a gut overgrowth (gut; Category 3 — may act on a root cause, but carries real downside)

*What it is.* This means a defined course of something intended to knock back an overgrowth of microbes in the small intestine — either a prescription antibiotic (a drug that kills or stops bacteria) or a strong herbal antimicrobial (concentrated plant extracts — for example berberine, oregano, or allicin preparations — used at doses meant to suppress microbes, not the trace amounts in food). The two are not identical, but they share the same logic and the same category of risk, so they are treated together here.

*Why this sits in the higher-risk group, not the low-risk one.* Three reasons, each its own kind of downside.

First, it acts on the whole neighbourhood, not just the target. The gut holds a large mixed community of microbes, and the helpful, protective majority — the ones that crowd out troublemakers and keep the lining fed — provide what is called colonisation resistance: a settled community is hard for an outsider to invade. An antimicrobial does not read name-tags. Clearing the target also thins the protective majority, and after the course ends recovery is a race for the emptied space. Fast-growing, oxygen-tolerant bacteria (the *Enterobacteriaceae* group — *E. coli*, *Klebsiella* and relatives) and any survivors tend to rebound first and overshoot before the slower protective community catches up. That window is exactly when an unwanted configuration can re-establish — so a course can, in some people, leave the community in a worse state than it started.

Second, the direct side-effects. Antibiotics carry their own load — looser stool, the risk of an opportunistic overgrowth (for example *Clostridioides difficile*, a bacterium that can bloom once the competition is removed), and drug-specific effects. Strong herbal antimicrobials are not automatically gentle: at suppressive doses they can irritate the gut lining and have their own interactions.

Third — and this is the one most often missed — it frequently *disappoints*, and the reasons are mechanical. A course only works where the drug reaches an effective concentration, against an organism the drug actually covers. Two ways it misses: wrong target, and wrong place. *Wrong target:* antibacterials do not touch fungi or archaea (a separate domain of single-celled microbes) at all — so a course aimed at bacteria leaves methane-making archaea, the organisms behind methane-predominant overgrowth, completely untouched, and removing their bacterial competition can even let them expand. *Wrong place:* a drug only kills where it arrives. A well-absorbed antibiotic taken by mouth is largely taken up high in the gut and very little intact drug reaches further down — so organisms living lower see far less than a blood level suggests. And even where the drug does reach, microbes living in a biofilm (a matrix-encased, slow-growing film on the mucus and surfaces) are phenotypically tolerant — not genetically resistant, but shielded and slow-dividing, so the same cells the drug touches are often not finished off, and regrow afterwards. The net picture is partial kills plus refilling: the overgrowth dips and then comes back, which is why repeating ever-harder courses so often fails while the underlying setup is unaddressed. [T1/T2] So the reward is real *only when* the target, the location, and the form are right — which is precisely what the tests below try to establish before taking on the downside.

### Cheap / at-home first: a structured fortnight of changed meal-spacing plus elimination-and-reintroduction of fermentable carbohydrate

*What it is.* A deliberate two-week trial run before anything is killed: (1) lengthening the gaps between meals (no grazing — letting the gut's between-meal "housekeeper" wave run, see below), and (2) removing the fermentable carbohydrates that gut microbes feed on most readily, then reintroducing them in a structured way while keeping a symptom-and-food diary. Fermentable carbohydrates are the sugars and fibres that microbes ferment into gas — the FODMAP group and similar.

*(1) The decision its result would change.* Whether **food is the main fuel for the gas** — and therefore whether a substrate-focused approach (changing what is eaten and when) should be tried *before* any antimicrobial at all. If symptoms fall away when the fuel is removed, the case for taking on the downside of a kill weakens considerably; the lever may be the diet, not the drug.

*(2) The node it addresses.* The **fermentation / substrate step** — the point where microbes meet fermentable material and turn it into gas. (The meal-spacing arm also speaks to a second node: between meals the small intestine runs a cyclical sweeping wave, the migrating motor complex, that clears residue and microbes downstream roughly every 90–120 minutes; constant snacking switches it off, so longer gaps let the sweep run. [T1]) Both are upstream of any overgrowth: less fuel and a working sweep mean less gas and less proximal accumulation, without killing anything.

*(3) Which result tilts toward what, and why.*
- A **clear improvement** when fermentable carbohydrate is removed *could possibly suggest* the gas is **fuel-driven** — because taking the substrate away took the source away. If the gas tracks the food that closely, the rate-limiting input is what is being fed in, and a substrate-and-timing approach may do most of the work an antimicrobial was being considered for, at a fraction of the risk.
- **Little change** despite a genuine fortnight of removal *could possibly suggest* the problem is less about fuel and more about **clearance or barrier** — the sweep being weak, or the lining over-reacting to what does cross — because removing the obvious fuel didn't remove the symptom. That doesn't *confirm* anything (the removal may have been incomplete, or the timing loose), but it lowers the weight on "just stop feeding them" and keeps the other nodes in play. [probabilistic — directional, not diagnostic]

*The honest limit of this test.* Diet trials are unblinded and confounded — expectation, natural week-to-week fluctuation, and the difficulty of removing one thing cleanly all muddy the read. A washout and a repeat make it more trustworthy than a single pass. It narrows the picture; it does not settle it.

### More expensive / specialist: a breath test (and, where available, a duodenal aspirate)

*What it is.* A breath test measures the gases — hydrogen and methane — you exhale over a few hours after a measured sugar drink; rises reflect microbial fermentation in the upper-to-mid small intestine. A duodenal aspirate is a fluid sample drawn from the upper small intestine during an endoscopy and cultured — the historical reference method, because that is the part of the gut an endoscope can physically reach.

*The decision they would change.* **Whether there is an overgrowth at all, roughly what kind, and roughly where** — which together determine whether an antimicrobial is even reasonable, and if so which one. This is the test that most directly governs the risk-versus-reward of the higher-risk option.

*The node they address.* The overgrowth itself — its presence, its dominant gas-producing type, and its approximate location along the gut. Location matters because, as above, a well-absorbed oral drug reaches the upper gut far better than the lower; and the small intestine keeps microbial density low at the top and rising toward the bottom, so "where" changes what is sensible. [T1 for the gradient]

*Which result tilts toward what, and why.*
- A **methane-positive pattern** *could possibly suggest* **methane-making archaea** (intestinal methanogen overgrowth) rather than hydrogen-making bacteria — a genuinely different target, because, as noted, ordinary antibacterials don't touch archaea. Methane-predominant overgrowth is associated with slowed transit and constipation, and the proposed mechanism — methane acting locally on the gut wall to increase non-propelling squeezing while slowing net forward movement — is shown in animal and organ-bath work but extrapolated to humans, so treat the *causal* story as a candidate rather than settled. [T2 for the association; T3 for the human-transit mechanism] The practical point stands regardless: a methane signal points the choice toward a different agent (or combination) than a hydrogen signal, and away from a bacteria-only course that would predictably disappoint.
- A **flat result** — little hydrogen, little methane, symptoms notwithstanding — *lowers the case for an antimicrobial at all*, because the thing a kill is meant to reduce isn't clearly evident. That tilts attention back toward the substrate, motility, and barrier nodes the at-home trial probes, where the risk-versus-reward is far gentler. [probabilistic]

*The honest limits.* Breath tests are imperfect — fast transit can give a false positive (the sugar reaches the colon and ferments there), and the cut-offs are debated. The aspirate samples only the upper gut, so a *lower* overgrowth can be missed entirely; a clean upper sample does not rule out an overgrowth sitting further down where the endoscope can't reach. Both narrow the odds; neither is a perfect map. [T2/T3]

*The record.* Why for this person: [overgrowth-pattern evidence, from the working-truth]. Already tried a kill before: [result — and if so, whether it disappointed, which itself is data on target/location/form]. Constraint check: [drug interactions; the user's documented hard-no list for any herbal components]. Sequencing note: the at-home fortnight is the cheaper, lower-risk first move; the breath test/aspirate is what would justify — and shape — an antimicrobial if the gentler route doesn't resolve it.

## Example B — A trial of thyroid hormone for a low-active-thyroid-hormone pattern (endocrine; Category 3 — may act on a root cause, but carries real downside)

*What it is.* This means taking thyroid hormone — usually T3, the active form, or a T4/T3 combination — to try to lift a pattern where the active hormone reaching the tissues looks low even though the standard thyroid screen reads roughly normal. To set up why this is higher-risk, the chain first.

*The chain, in plain terms.* The thyroid gland mostly puts out **T4 (thyroxine)**, a relatively *inactive* prohormone, plus a little **T3 (triiodothyronine)**, the *active* hormone that switches on the body's metabolic set-point. Most active T3 is actually made *outside* the thyroid, in tissues like the liver and kidney, by enzymes called deiodinases that strip one iodine off T4 to make T3 (the activation step). A third deiodinase instead diverts T4 down an off-ramp to **reverse T3 (rT3)**, an inactive dead-end. So the amount of active hormone you actually run on depends heavily on this **T4-to-T3 conversion step**, not just on what the gland secretes. [T1]

*Why this sits in the higher-risk group.* Two distinct downsides.

First, the direct effects of taking the active hormone. T3 raises the metabolic set-point body-wide, and overshooting has real consequences — it can disturb heart rhythm (the heart is sensitive to thyroid hormone, and excess raises the risk of an irregular fast rhythm, atrial fibrillation) and, over time, accelerate bone loss. T3 in particular acts fast and is less forgiving than T4. These are not trivial, reversible-in-an-afternoon effects.

Second — and this is the subtler trap — the low-T3 pattern *may not be a defect to correct at all.* During illness, severe stress, inflammation, or under-eating, the body deliberately turns conversion down and the off-ramp up: less T4 becomes active T3, more becomes inactive rT3. This is called non-thyroidal illness, or low-T3 syndrome, and it is widely read as an *adaptive* response — the body dialling its metabolic rate down on purpose while it is under strain. If that is what is happening, pushing active hormone back up could override a protective brake and do harm rather than good. [T1 for the enzymology; the adaptive reading is established and is the heart of the caution] So the reward depends entirely on *which* of these two situations is true — genuine impaired conversion that might benefit, versus an adaptive response that should be left alone — and that is exactly what needs establishing first. This is not a self-directed trial: because of the cardiac and bone downside and the adaptive-response ambiguity, any trial here belongs in a **clinician-supervised conversation**, not a solo experiment.

### Tests and clinician conversation first: free T3 and reverse T3 measured together

*What it is.* A blood panel that measures **free T3** (the active hormone available to tissues) and **reverse T3** (the inactive off-ramp product) *together* — ideally alongside free T4 and the standard TSH — so the *pattern* across them can be read, not any single number. Free means the unbound, usable fraction, as opposed to the portion carried inert on blood proteins.

*The decision its result would change.* **Whether the pattern is genuinely impaired conversion versus an adaptive response to illness** — and therefore whether a clinician-supervised hormone trial is even a reasonable thing to discuss. This is the gate. It does not by itself prescribe treatment; it decides whether treatment is a sensible conversation to have at all.

*The node it addresses.* The **T4-to-T3 conversion step** — directly. TSH alone speaks to what the pituitary senses, and the pituitary makes its own local T3 and can be relatively well-supplied while the liver and muscle run low — which is exactly why a normal TSH can sit on top of low tissue-level T3, and why the conversion step needs looking at on its own terms. [T1]

*Which result tilts toward what, and why.*
- **Low free T3 with a high reverse T3** *could possibly suggest* the body is actively shunting T4 down the inactive off-ramp — the signature of the **adaptive, non-thyroidal-illness** pattern — because if conversion to the active form were simply failing you would not expect the *inactive* product to be running high at the same time. High rT3 implies the off-ramp enzyme is up, which is the body's stress/illness setting. That tilt *weakens* the case for adding hormone, because it points to a protective brake rather than a fixable shortfall — the more so if there is an obvious ongoing stressor (illness, under-eating, inflammation) driving it. [probabilistic]
- **Low free T3 without a high reverse T3** (and with the rest of the picture fitting) *could possibly suggest* a conversion problem that is less clearly adaptive — and *that* is the version where a clinician might reasonably weigh a supervised trial, because the inactive off-ramp isn't the explanation. Even here it is a conversation, not a conclusion: the cardiac and bone downside still applies, and the underlying driver still matters. [probabilistic — directional, not diagnostic]

*The contested-evidence caveat, stated plainly.* Treating a low-T3 / high-rT3 pattern with thyroid hormone is **not** an established, settled intervention — for the non-thyroidal-illness picture especially, the evidence does not show it helps, and it may harm, precisely because the low-T3 state is often the adaptation rather than the disease. So the pattern is genuinely worth *understanding and tracking*, but "low T3 means take T3" is a contested, plausible-only proposition, not a proven fix. [T3 / contested] Any step from this panel toward an actual trial is a decision to make with a clinician who can weigh the heart-rhythm and bone risks, monitor, and reverse course — framed throughout as something to *discuss*, not something to do.

*The record.* Why for this person: [their symptom picture — fatigue, cold intolerance, dry skin, slowed cognition with a normal-looking TSH — from the working-truth]. Already on thyroid hormone or tested: [result]. Constraint check: [cardiac history; bone-density risk factors; current medications]. Sequencing note: the panel (free T3 + reverse T3, read together) is the gate, and it is a low-cost, low-risk first step; everything past it is a supervised clinical conversation, not an at-home trial.
