# Offer-example library — Section 2: Plausible narratives (the teaching core)

**Voice + bar (match this; do not copy the text).** Client-facing, plain-language, probabilistic. This is the deepest section of the whole offer, so it gets the most room. For a leading possibility, walk the *entire* chain from the first cause to the symptom you actually feel, in order — never skip a link. At each step do six things: (i) name the specific actor (the molecule, enzyme, gene, cell type, or family of microbes — not "the gut" or "hormones"); (ii) say what acts on what and in which direction, as a chain of verbs, never a bare outcome ("X switches on Y, which strips a piece off Z" — not "X causes the symptom"); (iii) where a molecule or gas then goes on to cause something downstream, explain *that* step and its inner workings too, don't hand-wave it; (iv) give the anatomical location of each step — which segment (stomach, duodenum, jejunum, ileum, colon) and which cell type, and for a hormone or enzyme, where it's *made* versus where it *acts*; (v) where a claim leans on an amount, say plainly whether that amount was measured or is an estimate, and whether it can realistically be reached inside the body; (vi) reconcile the obvious objection the person would raise — the "but that can't be right, because…" — honestly, not by waving it away. Give both a short summary and the full walk. Everything here is a candidate to discuss with a clinician — a way of understanding *how* something could be happening, never a statement that it *is*. De-prioritised, never ruled out. This is not a diagnostic conclusion. Two worked examples across domains follow (gut; endocrine).

## Example A — The cadaverine chain (gut): how a gut bug could be making an itch worse

**The short version.** Some gut bacteria, when they overgrow in the upper gut, make a molecule called cadaverine out of the protein in your food. Cadaverine and histamine — the molecule behind itch and hives — happen to be cleared from the gut by the same single enzyme. So a big load of cadaverine can keep that enzyme busy, leaving less of it free to mop up histamine, which then lingers and drives the itch. The competition between the two molecules for that one enzyme is well established. Whether bacterial cadaverine actually reaches a high enough level inside *your* gut to matter is not something anyone has measured — so this is a plausible candidate to consider, not a settled cause.

**The full walk.**

*Step 1 — the actor and where it lives.* Start with a specific group of bacteria: the gram-negative Enterobacteriaceae family. ("Gram-negative" is just a staining property that marks a broad class of bacteria; the family includes everyday names like *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, and *Enterobacter*.) These bugs carry an enzyme called **lysine decarboxylase**. An enzyme is a protein that runs one specific chemical reaction; this one's job is to act on the amino acid **lysine** — one of the building blocks of dietary protein. Normally the heavy bacterial activity in your gut lives far downstream, in the colon. But this particular trick is an Enterobacteriaceae specialty, and those are exactly the bugs that bloom higher up — in the stomach and the upper small intestine (the **duodenum** and **jejunum**, the first two segments after the stomach) — when the gut's upstream populations get out of balance (what's loosely called overgrowth, or SIBO). So the place where this runs isn't fixed by anatomy; it tracks wherever the bug has overgrown.

*Step 2 — what acts on what, and in which direction.* The enzyme strips the carboxyl group (a small carbon-and-oxygen cap) off lysine. That single edit turns lysine into a different molecule — a **diamine** called **cadaverine** — and releases a puff of carbon dioxide. (A diamine is just a molecule with two nitrogen-containing "amine" ends; the name is the one used for the smell of decay, which is the same chemistry.) Worth noting *why* the bug bothers: this reaction consumes acid, so the bacterium runs it to defend itself when its surroundings turn mildly acidic and lysine is on hand. That's why the output is greatest exactly where bacteria meet acid plus leftover protein — the upper gut after a protein meal. Direction so far: dietary protein → free lysine → bacterial enzyme acts → cadaverine.

*Step 3 — the clearing enzyme, and where it works.* Now the second actor: **diamine oxidase**, usually shortened to **DAO**. This is your *own* enzyme, made by the **enterocytes** — the cells that line the small intestine — and secreted toward the gut channel, where it works at the surface of the lining and in the lumen (the hollow space food passes through). DAO is the gut's frontline cleanup crew for amines, whether they come from food or from bacteria, breaking them down before they can cross into the bloodstream. The key fact: DAO does not clear only one molecule. Its targets include **histamine** *and* the diamines cadaverine and putrescine. One enzyme, several customers.

*Step 4 — the competition, and the downstream effect spelled out.* Because cadaverine and histamine are cleared by the *same* DAO, they compete for it. When a large load of cadaverine arrives, it occupies the enzyme, and there's less free DAO left to break down histamine. So histamine — which would normally be cleared quickly — instead lingers at the gut lining. Now follow that lingering histamine to the symptom, because this is the step most explanations skip. Histamine acts by docking onto **receptors** (proteins on a cell's surface that, when triggered, make the cell do something). The relevant ones here are **H1 receptors** on local sensory nerve endings, and **H1 and H4 receptors** on mast cells (immune cells that drive allergic-type reactions) and on the cells lining small blood vessels. When surplus histamine engages those receptors, the nerve endings fire as itch, the vessels leak and flush, and depending on where it happens you can get itch, redness, hives, looser stool, or headache. Full direction, end to end: more cadaverine → less free DAO for histamine → more surviving histamine → more receptor signalling → the felt itch.

*Step 5 — the honest part about amounts.* This is where it is worth pushing back, because this is the soft spot. The competition itself is solidly measured: in a controlled 2022 experiment, when cadaverine outnumbered histamine by about four to one, histamine breakdown fell by roughly a third; at about twenty to one, it fell by around eighty percent. So the *mechanism* of competition is real and quantified. But — and this matters — those numbers come from enzyme-and-food experiments in the lab, not from measuring the inside of a living person's gut. The open question is whether bacterially made cadaverine actually reaches that four-to-twenty-fold excess over histamine *at your gut lining, in real life*. In spoiled or fermented high-protein food the diamine load can get very high; from ordinary digestion, the level reached inside the gut is uncertain and probably varies a lot from person to person and day to day. Nobody has measured it in your gut. So the correct framing is: the enzyme-competition mechanism is established, but "your itch is caused by bacterial cadaverine out-competing your DAO" is a plausible candidate, not a proven cause — and the only way to know is to track and test, not to reason it into certainty.

*A note on genetic tests, since people ask.* Two different enzymes clear histamine in two different places: DAO does it outside cells, mainly in the gut lumen (the one that matters for food and bacterial histamine), while a second enzyme called HNMT clears histamine *inside* cells in tissues like the brain and liver. There are gene variants that can lower the activity of each. The catch worth stating plainly: consumer genotyping chips (the 23andMe-style kits) only read a sparse, fixed list of spots in these genes — they miss many functional variants, so a "normal" chip result does *not* rule out reduced DAO activity. A serum DAO activity test and a structured low-histamine elimination-then-rechallenge tell you more than the chip does here.

*Step 6 — reconciling the obvious objection.* The thoughtful objection is this: "If a gut bug out-competing my DAO were really driving my itch, then *every* high-protein meal should set it off, because lysine is in all protein — and an antihistamine should fully fix it. Neither is cleanly true for me. Doesn't that sink the whole idea?" It doesn't, and here's why, honestly:

- **Eating lysine is not the same as making cadaverine.** The conversion needs the bacteria to be present *and* the acid-plus-lysine conditions to switch the enzyme on *and* enough time for it to run. A clean, well-digested protein meal that's absorbed high up leaves little lysine behind for bacteria. A meal eaten when there's upper-gut overgrowth, slow transit, or incomplete digestion leaves leftover lysine for the bugs to work on. So *intermittent* symptoms — some meals, not others — are exactly what this mechanism predicts. It depends on a coincidence of bug, conditions, and timing, not on protein alone.
- **There are two separate problems: too much histamine, and too little clearance.** Cadaverine competition is only one input. Your total histamine load (from aged or fermented foods, or from mast cells releasing their own) and your baseline DAO capacity are others. Cadaverine matters *most* when your DAO is already near its limit — which is why two people eating the identical meal can react completely differently.
- **An antihistamine helping but not fixing it fits the story, rather than breaking it.** A peripheral antihistamine (for example fexofenadine, which doesn't enter the brain) blocks the H1 *receptor* — it does nothing to reduce the histamine load or restore your DAO. If part of the itch runs through H4 receptors, or is simply driven by sheer histamine excess, then partial relief is exactly what you'd expect. "It helps but doesn't fully fix it" is consistent with a histamine mechanism, not evidence against one.

*The honest limit, stated out loud.* Notice that this mechanism is flexible enough to explain both "symptoms track protein" and "symptoms don't track protein." That flexibility is precisely the warning sign: a story that can explain any outcome can't, on its own, tell you whether it's the *right* story for you. That's why the move here is not a confident verdict — it's a food-and-symptom diary plus single-ingredient rechallenge with a washout gap, which is the only thing that shows whether this is actually happening in your case. The mechanism explains *how* it could happen; only tracking shows *whether* it does. This is not a diagnostic conclusion.

*Optionally connected — the methane-and-motility thread.* If the picture also includes constipation or sluggish transit, there's a related chain worth holding open, because slow transit can feed the one above. Between meals, the small intestine runs a cyclical cleanup wave called the **migrating motor complex** — a "housekeeper" sweep, triggered largely by a hormone called **motilin** released from the lining of the duodenum and jejunum, that fires roughly every 90 to 120 minutes and physically pushes residue and bacteria downstream. It's one of the small bowel's main defences against overgrowth. Now the candidate insult: certain methane-producing microbes — not bacteria but **archaea**, dominated by *Methanobrevibacter smithii* — consume hydrogen and release **methane**. Methane appears to act *locally* on the gut muscle through the cholinergic (acetylcholine-using) nerve pathway, increasing squeezing-in-place contractions while *reducing* net forward movement — more squeezing, less moving — which slows transit. The honest caveat is the same shape as before: this was shown with high, artificial methane exposures in animal and isolated-tissue experiments, and no study has directly recorded the housekeeper wave's timing under methane in a living person. So "the methane in your gut is slowing your own cleanup wave" is an extrapolation, not a measurement. But if it *were* happening, slower transit would leave more leftover protein sitting beside upstream bacteria for longer — which loops straight back into Step 1 of the cadaverine chain, giving the bugs more substrate and more time. Two candidate processes that could reinforce each other, both to be tracked rather than assumed.

## Example B — The thyroid conversion chain (endocrine): tired, cold, and dry-skinned with a "normal" thyroid test

**The short version.** Your thyroid gland mostly puts out an *inactive* form of thyroid hormone. The active form is made later, elsewhere in the body, by converter enzymes that snip the inactive version into the active one. During illness, stress, or inflammation, the body turns those converters down and switches on a third enzyme that diverts the hormone into an inactive dead-end instead. The result can be genuine low-active-hormone symptoms — fatigue, feeling cold, dry skin — even while the standard thyroid blood test (TSH) reads normal, because that test mostly reflects what one small gland in the brain senses, and that gland makes its own active hormone locally. Two honest limits: this pattern is real, but it's also often the body's *deliberate* adaptation to being unwell — so it's something to understand and track, not automatically something to treat.

**The full walk.**

*Step 1 — the gland and what it actually secretes.* The **thyroid gland**, in the front of the neck, secretes mostly **T4** (thyroxine). The crucial and under-appreciated fact: T4 is largely a *prohormone* — a relatively inactive stored form. It's not the molecule that does the work in your cells. The thyroid releases only a little of the truly active hormone directly.

*Step 2 — where the active hormone is actually made, and by what.* Most of the active hormone, **T3** (triiodothyronine), is produced *outside* the thyroid, in peripheral tissues, by a family of converter enzymes called **deiodinases** ("de-iodine-ase" — enzymes that remove one iodine atom). Two of them do the activating step, T4 → T3, and they live in different places: **DIO1**, mainly in the **liver and kidney**, and **DIO2**, in the **brain, the pituitary gland, muscle**, and brown fat. T3 is the molecule that enters the cell nucleus and actually sets the metabolic pace. So the supply of active hormone to your tissues depends not just on the gland, but on these converters doing their job. Direction: thyroid → T4 (inactive) → deiodinase removes one iodine → T3 (active) → binds receptors in the cell → metabolic effect.

*Step 3 — the off-ramp, named and located.* There's a third converter, **DIO3**, and it does the opposite. Instead of making active T3, it diverts T4 into **reverse T3** (rT3) — a mirror-image fragment that is *inactive* and does no metabolic work. Think of DIO3 as the off-ramp: hormone goes in, nothing useful comes out the other side. In a balanced state, activation (DIO1/DIO2) and the off-ramp (DIO3) sit in a workable proportion.

*Step 4 — what acts on the converters, and in which direction.* Here's where it can go wrong. During illness, prolonged stress, under-eating, or inflammation — a state doctors call **non-thyroidal illness** or "low-T3 syndrome" — the body shifts the balance: it turns *down* the activating converters (DIO1 and DIO2) and turns *up* the off-ramp (DIO3). The consequence follows directly: less T4 becomes active T3, and more T4 becomes inactive reverse T3. So the level of active hormone reaching your tissues drops, even though the gland may be producing T4 perfectly well. (A side note that's sometimes a real lever: these converters are selenium-dependent — they're built with selenium at their working core — so selenium status is a genuine input, though in someone already well-nourished it's usually a small factor.)

*Step 5 — the felt symptom.* Active T3 sets the metabolic "thermostat" inside target tissues. When tissue T3 falls, that thermostat drops, and the felt result is **fatigue, feeling cold when others don't, constipation, dry skin, slowed thinking, and a stalled metabolic rate** — the classic underactive-thyroid feeling. The twist is that this can happen while a standard thyroid screen looks reassuring, because the problem is in the *peripheral conversion* step, not necessarily in the gland's output. Whether and how much this should be treated is genuinely contested — see the limit at the end.

*Step 6 — reconciling the obvious objection.* The natural objection: "But my thyroid blood test was *normal* — my doctor checked my TSH and it was fine. So this can't be a thyroid problem; it must be something else." This is the most important step, and the answer is specific. **TSH** (thyroid-stimulating hormone) is made by the **pituitary**, a small gland at the base of the brain, and it reflects what *the pituitary* senses about thyroid supply — it's a feedback signal, not a direct readout of every tissue. Here's the catch: the pituitary is one of the tissues that makes its *own* active T3 locally, using DIO2 right there on site. So the pituitary can be comfortably supplied with T3 — and therefore report "all fine" by keeping TSH normal — *while* the liver and muscle are running low on T3. The brain's local supply masks the body's peripheral shortfall. That's why, when conversion is the suspected issue, a TSH alone can read normal and still miss it; looking at **free T4, free T3, and reverse T3 together** is more informative than TSH by itself. A normal TSH does not, on its own, prove your tissues are getting enough thyroid action.

*The honest limit, stated out loud.* There's a second objection that deserves equal honesty, and it cuts the *other* way: just because you *can* find a low-T3, high-reverse-T3 pattern doesn't mean it should be treated. In non-thyroidal illness, the body's down-shifting of conversion is often an **adaptive** response — a sensible way to lower the metabolic rate and conserve resources while you're unwell — not the disease itself. Giving thyroid hormone to "correct" that pattern is *not* established to help and can be harmful. So the right stance is to *understand and track* this pattern, and to investigate what's driving it (the underlying illness, stress, or inflammation), rather than to read "low T3" as an automatic instruction to take T3. This is a way of making sense of how a normal test and real symptoms can coexist — a candidate to explore with a clinician, not a diagnostic conclusion, and not a treatment recommendation.
