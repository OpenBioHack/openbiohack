# The N=1 protocol — running a careful self-experiment

Anything you decide to trial — a supplement, a food change, a lifestyle change — is worth treating as a
small, deliberate experiment. The point is to learn what's actually true *for your body*, not to confirm
what you were hoping. The discipline below is what separates a real signal from placebo and from normal
day-to-day noise.

This reference is about **method**. *What* to trial comes from the investigation's offering (low-risk,
reversible options, ranked) discussed with your clinician — never from this document.

## Before you start

1. **Establish a baseline.** Track the metric you care about for ~1 week under stable conditions, with no
   other changes. Without a baseline, "I feel better" is indistinguishable from placebo.
2. **Write the prediction down first.** State, before starting: *if the working idea is right, I'd expect
   to see X, because Y.* Pre-registering the prediction is what stops you from rationalizing whatever
   happens after the fact. When prediction matches reality, the idea gains weight; when it doesn't, the
   mismatch is the single most valuable data point — it means the working model is wrong somewhere.
3. **Set your thresholds in advance.** Decide *now* what counts as success (a specific, measurable change),
   what would make you pause and reassess, and what would make you stop and seek advice. Deciding these
   while you're in the middle of a reaction is too late.

## While you're running it

4. **Change one variable at a time.** If you change diet *and* add a supplement together, no result can be
   attributed to either. One change, one window.
5. **Track daily**: the target metric, the dose actually taken, and the confounders (sleep, stress, diet
   deviations, other symptoms). Confounders are not clutter — they're how you avoid crediting a change to
   the wrong cause.
6. **Give it long enough.** Typical minimums: supplements and dietary changes ~2–4 weeks; probiotics ~4–6
   weeks; lifestyle changes ~4–8 weeks. Effects accumulate and the body adapts.

## After

7. **Judge against the threshold you set in advance.** If the change is smaller than your normal day-to-day
   variation, it's noise — not a result. Be willing to call it that.
8. **Wash out before the next one.** ~1–2 weeks off before starting a different experiment (unless you're
   continuing something that's working well by your pre-set threshold), so the next baseline is clean.
9. **Update the picture.** Record what this means for the working understanding: which candidate
   possibilities it raises, which it lowers, and by roughly how much. Log it in `experiment-log.md`.

## Two honest calibrations

- **A mismatch beats a match.** A result that contradicts the prediction is more informative than one that
  fits, because it forces the model to change. Don't bury "it didn't work" — that *is* the finding.
- **Optimization signals are subtle.** When you're raising a ceiling rather than fixing something broken,
  a genuine 10–20% improvement is a real success and easy to miss without deliberate measurement. Prefer
  objective metrics (resting HR / HRV, sleep stages, a tracked lift or run time, before/after blood work)
  over pure self-report, and consider blinding yourself (e.g. unmarked capsules alternating with placebo
  weeks) when the effect you're chasing is small.

## Safety stays in front

The watch-points you set in step 3 are where safety lives. Anything in the "stop and seek advice" band —
a new symptom, a strong reaction, anything that needs medical attention — means you stop and talk to your
clinician, regardless of what the experiment was testing. The experiment is always subordinate to that.
