# Optimization mode — raising a ceiling instead of fixing a floor

This lens applies when someone is *functional* and wants to move toward *thriving* — more durable energy,
sharper cognition, faster recovery, longer healthspan. It is **not a separate product**. It runs the same
machinery as the investigation engine (mechanism understanding, the research sub-skills, N=1 experiments,
the ongoing logs, the council, the register). What changes is the **thrust**:

- **Investigation** works *backward*: "what process is producing this symptom?"
- **Optimization** works *forward*: "here is the system you want to optimize — what are all the mechanisms
  it depends on, and where could you *safely* influence them?"

The continuum matters: most people are some of both. You can fix a floor in one domain while raising a
ceiling in another — say so when you do.

The register still applies in full, even though the stakes are lower. Output stays possibility-framed:
*candidate levers to trial as N=1, discussed with your clinician* — never "take creatine." One honest
calibration to preserve: for **low-risk, reversible** interventions in **healthy** people, the
risk/benefit is favorable, so the evidence bar for *trialing* (not for *claiming an effect*) is
reasonably relaxed. That relaxation is about willingness to experiment, never about overstating what's
known — claims still carry their honest tier.

## The four-layer ceiling model (the optimization prioritizer)

For any performance domain (energy, cognition, recovery, mood, body composition…), four layers limit
performance. They're addressed **in order** — work on a higher layer is wasted while a lower one is
unaddressed.

```
LAYER 4 — ENHANCEMENT
  Levers that push beyond your baseline: specific supplements, cold/heat,
  targeted practices. Only meaningful once Layers 1–3 are solid.
      ↑
LAYER 3 — GENETIC CEILING
  Your hardware has limits — enzyme activity, receptor density, transport
  capacity. You can't change these, but you can work *around* them
  (e.g. methylated B vitamins for an MTHFR variant). See §genetics.
      ↑
LAYER 2 — FOUNDATION
  Sleep (7–9h, consistent), whole-food diet, regular movement, stress
  regulation, hydration, daylight. For most people this is where ~80% of
  the available gain actually lives.
      ↑
LAYER 1 — DEFICIENCY / HIDDEN PROBLEM
  Something is low or broken: vitamin D at 15 ng/mL, iron deficiency,
  undertreated thyroid, undiagnosed celiac, a chronic infection. These
  cap everything above them. Surface and address these first.
```

How to use it:
1. For each domain, work out which layer the person is *actually* at.
2. Don't surface Layer-4 levers for someone sitting at Layer 2. The honest conversation is: *"a NAD⁺
   precursor is a candidate worth discussing — but you're sleeping 5.5 hours and eating mostly processed
   food, and closing those two would plausibly do far more than any supplement. Worth weighing where to
   put the effort first."*
3. When Layers 1–2 are genuinely solid, Layer-4 levers become worth a careful trial.

### Layer assessment

**Layer 1 — deficiencies / hidden problems.** Review blood work the person has (vitamin D, B12, ferritin,
thyroid panel, fasting insulin, HbA1c, homocysteine, RBC magnesium). If genetic data exists, check for
absorption/metabolism variants that create hidden shortfalls despite "adequate" intake. Any clinically low
marker is the priority — and if optimization assessment *uncovers* a Layer-1 problem, that domain switches
to the investigation lens (fix the floor first, then optimize), noted in the picture.

**Layer 2 — foundations.** Score each 1–10 through conversation: sleep (hours, consistency, waking rested),
diet (whole foods, protein, micronutrients, timing), movement (≥150 min/week moderate + some strength),
stress (manageable, with recovery), basics (hydration, daylight, social connection). Anything below ~7/10
is a candidate target — and almost always a higher-leverage one than a supplement.

**Layer 3 — genetic.** Where genetics create specific needs, work around them (the genetics protocol in the
orchestrator governs raw-data verification). Examples: MTHFR → methylated B vitamins; VDR/GC → vitamin D
dose calibrated to blood work; COMT-slow → caution with methyl-donors and catechol load. Permanent hardware
constraints — optimize around them, don't fight them.

**Layer 4 — enhancement.** Only once 1–3 are addressed. Candidate levers (examples, not prescriptions) live
in `high-value-levers.md`, each to consider and trial as N=1 per `n1-protocol.md`, discussed with a clinician.

## The forward trace (desired state → requirements → bottleneck → safe lever)

The optimization counterpart to the engine's upstream-cascade. Instead of tracing from a symptom down to a
cause, trace from a **desired state** down to its biological requirements, then find the bottleneck and the
*safest* place to influence it.

```
DESIRED STATE: durable energy across the whole day
  ↓ requires
efficient mitochondrial function (electron transport, NAD⁺, CoQ10)
  ↓ requires
adequate substrate + delivery (B vitamins, iron, magnesium, O₂)
  ↓ requires
proper absorption (gut function, stomach acid, bile)
  ↓ rests on
foundation: sleep (mitochondrial repair), diet (substrate), movement
            (mitochondrial biogenesis)
  ↓ checked against
genetic variants affecting any node in the chain
```

Map the requirements for the specific domain, identify which one is most likely underperforming for *this*
person, and that bottleneck is where a candidate lever would be aimed. The research sub-skills
(`/research`, `/research-practitioner`) do the mechanism depth; the engine's machinery runs the rest.

## Tracking optimization (effects are subtle)

Raising a ceiling produces smaller signals than fixing something broken — a genuine 10–20% gain is real and
easy to miss. Lean on objective metrics and on the N=1 discipline:

- **Objective (prefer these):** resting HR + HRV, sleep stages, a tracked lift / run time / recovery HR,
  before-and-after blood panels (~3 months apart), body composition where relevant.
- **Subjective (useful, placebo-prone):** energy / focus / mood / soreness on a 0–10 scale, logged at the
  same time of day.
- **Control the placebo:** pre-commit to a threshold ("I'll conclude it helped *only* if [metric] moves by
  [amount]"); consider blinding yourself with unmarked capsules alternating with placebo weeks; minimum
  ~4-week trials since effects accumulate and adapt.

Optimization-target entries in the picture look like: current level (X/10); the biological requirements of
the domain; the current bottleneck; the layer (1/2/3/4); a *candidate* lever aimed at the bottleneck; a
*realistic* expected change ("6 → 7.5/10", not "6 → 10/10"); and how it'll be measured.

## Optimization anti-patterns (in addition to the general ones in the orchestrator)

1. **Supplement-first thinking** — reaching for pills before sleep/diet/movement/stress. Layer 2 is ~80% of
   the gain for most people; supplements are the remaining ~20%, and only once foundations hold.
2. **Chasing marginal gains on broken foundations** — a NAD⁺ precursor while sleeping 5 hours is racing fuel
   in a car with flat tyres. Fix the tyres first.
3. **Confusing popular with effective** — trending on a podcast is not evidence. Same evidence hierarchy
   applies; most hyped compounds have weak human data.
4. **Not knowing your baseline** — "I feel better" without a baseline is indistinguishable from placebo.
   Measure before and after, objectively where you can.
5. **Expecting drug-sized effects from supplements** — they optimize within the normal range; they don't
   transform a healthy person. A 10–20% domain improvement is a genuine win. If someone expects more, either
   recalibrate, or look harder for a hidden Layer-1 problem.
6. **Ignoring diminishing returns** — the first lever in a domain gives the biggest return, the fifth almost
   nothing. Know when "good enough" is reached and redirect effort to a different domain.
